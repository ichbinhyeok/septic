# Support State Research

Last updated: 2026-03-09

## Purpose

This note upgrades the second-wave V1 states so they are not empty placeholders.
The goal is not fake precision. The goal is to publish safer homeowner guidance with:

- a real state-specific angle
- a visible permit path
- a visible local-override note
- official source provenance

## Massachusetts

- Why it matters: Title 5 creates a strong home-sale and addition trigger that national generic cost pages usually ignore.
- Publishable angle: inspection timing, Board of Health workflow, and property-transfer risk matter as much as raw install cost.
- Official signals used:
  - Title 5 overview hub
  - buying or selling property with a septic system
  - Title 5 regulation page
- Safe public takeaways:
  - Title 5 inspections are generally tied to property transfer timing.
  - Annual pumping can extend inspection validity in the specific cases described by the state.
  - Adding a bedroom or changing use can trigger more review.

## Washington

- Why it matters: local health jurisdictions are the real execution layer, and Washington openly explains system types plus inspection cadence.
- Publishable angle: homeowners need to know who the LHJ is before trusting any quote or permit assumption.
- Official signals used:
  - statewide OSS hub
  - management roles page
  - caring for your septic system
  - types of septic systems
- Safe public takeaways:
  - LHJs issue permits and can be more protective than the statewide code.
  - Gravity systems and advanced systems have different inspection cadence.
  - System type explanations are strong enough to support better risk messaging.

## Florida

- Why it matters: the 2025 DEP transfer changed the homeowner permit path and creates a real state-specific trust angle.
- Publishable angle: first identify whether the county is DEP-managed or still routed through the county health department.
- Official signals used:
  - Florida DOH onsite sewage and septic hub
  - construction approvals page
  - homebuyer's guide PDF
- Safe public takeaways:
  - Since 2025-01-02, DEP directly manages septic permitting in 16 counties.
  - Outside those counties, county health departments still handle permitting and inspections.
  - Inspection and approval sequence matters more than pretending there is one simple statewide sizing table.

## Missouri

- Why it matters: Missouri clearly explains when state minimums apply, when local rules can be stricter, and when a permit is required.
- Publishable angle: permit authority and site/soil-based system type are the main homeowner risks.
- Official signals used:
  - OWTS program page
  - construction permit process page
  - onsite resources page
- Safe public takeaways:
  - The state minimum can be overridden by stricter local rules.
  - Major changes like tank or field replacement are part of the public standards context.
  - Permit triggers should be treated as part of the estimate checklist.

## North Carolina

- Why it matters: the permit ladder is clear and gives the site a non-thin angle even before exact sizing logic is fully extracted.
- Publishable angle: improvement permit -> construction authorization -> operation permit or certificate of completion.
- Official signals used:
  - On-Site Water Protection Branch
  - 18E resources
  - program resources page
- Safe public takeaways:
  - Local health departments control the practical homeowner path.
  - The branch publishes the permit forms and resources directly.
  - Larger systems move into a more technical design and review path.

## New Jersey

- Why it matters: New Jersey has a publishable non-thin angle once the page is framed around management obligations and local oversight instead of pretending there is a clean statewide size table.
- Publishable angle: local board of health control, pump-out/inspection expectations, and Pinelands advanced-system obligations.
- Official signals used:
  - NJDEP septic systems page
  - Pinelands septic system management page
  - NJDEP septic management model ordinance
  - Pinelands alternate design septic system program
- Safe public takeaways:
  - Local management and board of health reporting matter.
  - Pump-out and inspection obligations are part of the homeowner cost story.
  - Sensitive-area and advanced-system rules can create long-tail maintenance cost that a generic install page misses.

## Still Missing

- Tank-size exactness is still intentionally weak in these support states.
- If a state lacks a clean public sizing table, keep the calculator conservative and emphasize process risk.
